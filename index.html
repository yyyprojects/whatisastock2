WHAT IS A STOCK?

An Illustrated Guide to Owning a Piece of a Company

It All Starts With a Simple Idea

To understand stocks, forget Wall Street and think about a pizza. A company can be sliced up just like a pizza to raise money for growth. Each slice is a real piece of ownership.

Why Companies Sell Stock

The primary reason is to raise **capital** (money) to fund expansion, innovate new products, and hire talent without taking on debt.

Your Protection: Limited Liability

As a shareholder, you are not personally responsible for the company's debts. The most you can lose is the amount you invested.

The Two Faces of Stock

Not all stock is created equal. Companies issue two main types, Common and Preferred, which offer different balances of risk, reward, and control to investors.

How You Make (or Lose) Money

There are two main ways to profit: Dividends (a share of profits paid to you) and Capital Gains (selling your stock for more than you paid).

Investor Shorthand: Stock Categories

Investors use labels to quickly describe a stock's investment profile. These aren't rigid rules, but helpful categories.

üöÄ Growth Stocks

Fast-growing companies that reinvest profits to expand. The bet is on future price increases, not current income.

üíé Value Stocks

Stocks that seem undervalued by the market. Investors buy them hoping the price will rise to its true worth.

üí∞ Income Stocks

Stable companies that pay regular dividends, providing a steady income stream for investors.

üèÜ Blue-Chip Stocks

Large, well-known, financially sound companies with a history of reliable performance.

The Market Guardian: The SEC

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is a government agency whose mission is to protect investors, maintain fair markets, and ensure companies disclose key financial information to the public.
